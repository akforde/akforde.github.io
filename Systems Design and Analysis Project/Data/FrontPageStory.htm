Staff correspondent

April 30, 2013 - Futureville city council last night approved city participation in a car sharing operation designed to address a number of the city's transportation woes.

As part of a municipal transportation strategy, city council committed to a forward-looking transportation policy, of which the first stage is a car sharing system. Council approved a special budget measure that allow the city's departments of Transportation and Environmental Quality to finance a three-year pilot car sharing project that will be operated out of city facilities. Councillor Darnell Waistcoat, responsible for Transportation, stated that "the initiative is an integral part of the city's transportation future." When asked about the cost he stated that "all experiences with car sharing show that it is profitable within 2-3 years. It's the right idea at the right time!"

Car sharing is to automobiles what time-sharing is to resort properties - a formal arrangement by a group of people to share a resource. Many people do not need a car at all times, and their household's first or second vehicle may not be used much. Being able to use a car "part-time" may meet their mobility needs at a lower cost to themselves and society.

For those driving less than the average number of miles a year, car sharing is less expensive than owning a car. Members in car sharing organizations tend to use transit, walk, or bicyce for a significant share of their transportation needs. Car sharing allows the added option of a car to these transportation choices.

Councillor Waistcoat was delighted that the measure had finally passed. "This starts to address the increasing traffic congestion and parking problems that are blighting the city experience for many people," he said.

Car sharing is expected to result in lower vehicle ownership rates and lower overall vehicle distance traveled. In neighborhoods where public parking is severely limited, fewer vehicles on the street will improve the parking situation. Lower overall vehicle distance traveled will result in reduced road congestion and in lower overall vehicle emissions.

The Transportation department also committed to disposing of some of its fleet of passenger vehicles and using car share vehicles to fill the gap. "It's a win-win situation," said Councillor Wilbur Jenkins. "The costs of car sharing are clearly less than those of ownership below a certain level of usage."

Councillor James Bright was also enthusiastic about the possibilities. "We may consider providing incentives for incorporating car sharing in future housing developments," the councillor stated. "This is as important as energy-efficient housing standards, and will appeal to a new generation of renters and condo owners."
